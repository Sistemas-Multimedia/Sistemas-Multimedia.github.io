1 Description

1.1 The IPP... decorrelation pattern

It’s time to put together all the “tools” that we have developed for encoding a sequence of frames {V k}. First, the sequence will be splitted into GOFs (Group Of Frames), and the structure of each GOF will be IPP... [1], which means that the ﬁrst frame of each GOF will be intra-coded (I-type), and the rest of frames of the GOF will be predicted-coded (P-type), respect to the previous one1 . Notice that in an I-type frame all the coeﬃcients (coeﬀs in short, remember that we are compensating the motion in the DWT domain) will be I-type coeﬀs, and in a P-type frame, the diﬀerent coeﬀs will be I-type or P-type.

1.2 A block diagram of the codec


Figure 1: The IPP... MRVC video codec.

The MRVC IPP... codec has been described in the Fig. 1. The equations that describe this system are:

(V k.L,V k.H) ←DWT(V k), (a)

where ← denotes the assignment operator, and V k is the k-th frame of the sequence,

[V k.L] ←DWT−1(V k.L,0), (E.a)
Z−1([V k.L]) = [V k−1.L], (E.b)

and by deﬁnition, Z−1([V −1.L]) = 0.

V k + 1 → k ←ME([V k.L],[V k−1.L]), (E.c)

where ME stands for Motion Estimation, and by deﬁnition, V 0 → (−1) = 0,

[V ̂k.L] ←MCP(V k + 1 → k,[V k−1.L]), (E.d)

where MCP stands for Motion Compensated Prediction,

[Ek.L] ← [V k.L] − [V ̂k.L], (E.e)
{[Mk],[Sk]}←EW-min([V k.L],[Ek.L]) (E.f)

where

[Mk]i,j = min([V k.L]i,j,[Ek.L]i,j), (1)

and [Sk] is a binary matrix deﬁned by

[Sk]i,j = 0 if[V k.L]i,j < [Ek.L]i,j (I-type coeﬀ) 1 otherwise (P-type coeﬀ), (2)

(notice that [Mk], that contains the element-wise minimum of both matrices, is discarded)

[V k.H] ←DWT−1(0,V k.H), (b)
[Ek.H] ← [V k.H] − [V ̂k.H], (c)

where, notice that

[Ek.H]i,j = [V k.H]i,j if[Ŵk.H]i,j = 0(I-type coeﬀ) [V k.H]i,j − [Ŵk.H]i,j otherwise(P-type coeﬀ), (3)
[Ẽk.H] ←Q([EK.H]), (d)
[Ẽk.H] ←Q−1([Ẽ K.H]), (E.g)
[Ṽk.H] ← [Ẽk.H] + [V ̂k.H], (E.h)

and notice that if [V ̂k.H] = 0 then [Ṽk.H] = [Ẽk.H],

Z−1([Ṽ k.H]) = [V k−1.H], (E.i)

and by deﬁnition, Z−1([V −1.H]) = 0,

[V ̂k.H] ←MCP(V k + 1 → k,[V k−1.H]), (E.j)
[Ŵk.H]i,j ← [V ̂k.H]i,j if[Ek.L]i,j < [V k.L]i,j(P-type coeﬀ) 0 otherwise (I-type coeﬀ), (E.k)
(0,Ẽk.H) ←DWT([Ẽk.H]), (f)
{V k.L,Ẽk.H}←E(V k.L,Ẽk.H), (g)

where E represents the entropy coding of both data sources, in two diﬀerent code-streams,

(V k.L,Ẽk.H) ←E−1({V k.L,Ẽk.H}), (h)
[Ẽk.H] ←DWT−1(0,Ẽ k.H), (i)
(0,Ṽk.H) ←DWT(0,[Ṽk.H]), (j)

and

Ṽk ←DWT−1(V k.L,Ṽk.H). (k)

1.3 Spatial multiresolution

As it can be seen in the previous section, in each IPP... iteration, only the high-frequency information of the sequence of frames is decorrelated (H subbands) considering the information provided by the low-frequences (L subband), which are losslessly transmitted between the encoder and the decoder. Notice that if the L data cannot be transmitted to the decoder, a drift error will occur because the matrix Sk will be diﬀerent in the encoder and the decoder for the same frame k.

Obviously (and unfortunately), the lossless transmission of the L’s bounds the compression ratio that we will get. One solution is to perform more (than 1) levels at the DWT stage (see Eq. (a)) and to apply the IPP... MRVC codec by spatial resolutions, starting at the lowest, as the decoder will do at decompression time. If we represent the Spatial Resolution Level (SRL) with an superindex, being 0 the original SRL, we can express the operation of the codec described in the Fig. 1 by

C(V k0) = {V k0.L,Ẽk0.H} = {V k1,Ẽk0.H} D({V k1,Ẽk0.H}) = Ṽk0, (4)

where C represents the IPP... encoder and D the IPP... decoder. As it can be seen, Eq. 4 is only valid when only one level of the DWT has been applied.

In general, for s levels of the DWT, we have that

C(V ks−1) = {V ks,Ẽks−1.H} D({V ks,Ẽks−1.H}) = Ṽks−1, (5)

where Ṽs−1 is the (s − 1)-th SRL of the reconstructed sequence Ṽ.

The next SRL (s − 2), Ṽs−2, is determined by

C(Ṽks−2) = {Ṽks−1,Ẽks−2.H} D({Ṽks−1,Ẽks−2.H}) = Ṽks−2, (6)

and ﬁnally, for the highest SRL, we get Ṽ0

C(Ṽk0) = {Ṽk1,Ẽk0.H} D({Ṽk1,Ẽk0.H}) = Ṽk0. (7)


Figure 2: Implementing spatial multiresolution in IPP... MRVC. Ts stands for the s-levels DWT.

So, only the lowest SRL of Ṽ, Ṽs is an III... pure sequence of small frames, losslessly encoded. This multiresolution (multistage) scheme has been described in the Fig. 2. The output of an IPP... Encoder iteration will be refered as “a spatial-layer of the code-stream”, or simply as “a S-layer”.

1.4 SQ (layers) progression

The logical order of the S-layers in the code-stream is the one that allows, when the code-stream is decoded sequentially, the progressive increase of the spatial resolution of the video. For example, if s is the number of levels of the DWT, the generated code-stream has (NSRL + 1) S-layers

{V s,Ẽs−1.H,Ẽs−2.H,⋯,Ẽ0.H},

which are able to generate (NSRL + 1) progressive reconstructions

{V s,Ṽs−1,Ṽs−2,⋯,Ṽ0}.

Moreover, Quality (Q-progression) scalability in each SRL can be also achieved in the high-frequency textures if a quality-scalable image codec such as JPEG2000 [2] replaces the PNG compressor, generating a number NQL of quality-layers (“Q-layers”) by each motion compensated high-frequency subband. A SQ-progression is deﬁned considering both forms of scalability (spatial and quality), with a higher number of layers. For example, if NSRL = 3 (2 IPP...-type iterations) and NQL = 2, the progression of layers would be

{V s[1],V s[0],Ẽs−1.H[1],Ẽs−1.H[0],Ẽs−2.H[1],Ẽs−2.H[0],⋯,Ẽ0.H[1],Ẽ0.H[0]}.

The use of quality scalability boosts the possibilities in real-time streaming scenarios where the transmission bit-rate can be variable (sending more or less Q-layers of a given spatial resolution depending on the bit-rate). Notice that the SQ-progression is free of drift-error.2
