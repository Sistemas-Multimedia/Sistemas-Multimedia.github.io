1 Description

Technically, the DWT (Discrete Wavelet Transform) is a linear basis expansion which compute a critically-sampled octave-band decomposition [3, ?].

1.1 Transform coding

Transform coding can exploit correlation in signals to concentrate the energy. Speciﬁcally, the DWT is a family of transforms whose output describes the (input) signal in a set of subbands. This set is also called a decomposition and the index of the subband is related to the frequency of the signal. For example, in the case of the images, the position of the coeﬃcients in the subbands is related to the spatial area where the related pixels are found.

All linear1 transforms can be described by matrix-vector product [2] as

y = Kx, (1)

where x is the input signal, K is the analysis transform matrix, and y is the output decomposition. Notice that

yi = ⟨Ki,xi⟩, (2)

where Ki is the i-th row of K, and ⟨⋅,⋅⟩ denotes the inner product. This basically means that yi is proportional to the similarity between the input signal x and the taps of the ﬁlter Ki. These slides can help you with this key idea. The inverse (synthesis) transform is computed by

x = K−1y, (3)

where K−1 denotes to the inverse matrix of K.

For example, the 2x2-KLT [1] is deﬁned by

[ y0 y1 ] = [ 1 2 1 2 1 2 −1 2 ] [ x0 x1 ] , (4)

and it holds that

K = K−1 = KT, (5)

where KT represents the transpose matrix of K. The Eq. 5 is true for all orthogonal transforms, and therefore

⟨Ki,Kj⟩ = 0,∀ ⁡i≠j. (6)

1.2 An example of DWT: the Haar DWT

As the rest of transforms, the DWT is also described by the ﬁlters of K. Haar deﬁned the analysis downsampling ﬁlters

[ lns+1 hns+1 ] = [ 1 2 1 2 1 2 −1 2 ] [ l2ns l2n+1s ] , (7)

where the superindex s denotes the subband, and n refers to the n-th element of the signal. By deﬁnition (and notice that this holds for all DWTs),

l0 = x. (8)

As it can be seen in the Fig. 1, K0 is a low-pass ﬁlter and K1 is a high-pass ﬁlter (this holds for all DWTs). Considering the Haar ﬁlter, we can conclude that:

  1. There exists aliasing between the ﬁlters (this holds for all DWTs) and this is a drawback because:
    1. The information with the same frequency can be found in both subbands (l and h). Therefore, the concentration of the energy in one of the subbands is smaller.
    2. At it can be seen also in Eq. 7, the subbands are downsampled by 2 and both subbands should have, at most, a bandwidth of 1∕2 radians/sample in order to avoid the aliasing during the subsampling, and increasing thus the perceptible quality of the low-pass subband.

    These problems can be only solved using ﬁlters that have transfer functions that overlaps a smaller area (see Figs. 2 and 3).

  2. There is not phase distortion (the phase of the ﬁlters is a linear function of the frequency, see the Fig. 4). This means that coeﬃcients lns+1 and hns+1 refers to the section of signal {l2ns,l2n+1s}, allowing the design of entropy codecs for the decomposition which exploit the correlation between subbands. Notice that this does not hold for all ﬁlters (see Fig. 5).


Figure 6: Computation of the S-levels DWT and the generated subbands.

Eq. 7 computes the 1-levels Haar DWT. A more general expresion for this equation is (and this holds for all DWTs)

{ls+1,hs+1} = DWT(ls), (9)

where {⋅,⋅} denotes the concatenation of subbands. As can be seen in the Fig. 6, it’s possible to compute the S-levels DWT, using Eq. 9 iteratively over the low-frequency subband, generating the decomposition

{l0S,h 0S,h 0S−1h 1S−1,h 0S−2h 1S−2h 2S−2h 3S−2,⋯,h 01h 11⋯h 2n−1−11} = DWTS(l0), (10)

where

n = log ⁡ 2(N) (11)

where N is the number of samples.

In the case of the Haar ﬁlters, the synthesis (inverse) 1-levels DWT (that we will denote by DWT−1) is the result of solving the coeﬃcients lns+1 and hns+1 in the Eq. 7. In general, for longer DWT ﬁlters we are going to have more coeﬃcients, but the same procedure can be used for ﬁnding the synthesis transform. Therefore, it can be written that

ls = DWT−1({ls+1,hs+1}), (12)

and DWT−S, the S-levels synthesis DWT, can be computed as it is describen in the Fig. 6, simply by reversing the analysis steps.

1.3 Multiresolution analysis

In the decomposition described in Eq. 10 there are S + 1 subbands, and therefore, it is possible to compute S + 1 resolution levels of the analyzed signal l0:

  • The resolution level S of l0 (no DWT−1) lS, with only one sample l0S that uses to be the arithmetic mean of l0. This coeﬃcient (and the only sample of lS), is also called the DC (Direct Current) coeﬃcient. On the contrary, the rest of coeﬃcents (high-frequency subbands) are called the AC (Alternating Current) coeﬃcients.
  • The resolution level S − 1 of l0, lS−1 = DWT−1({lS,hS}), with two samples.
  • The resolution level S − 1 of l0, lS−2 = DWT−1({lS−1,hS−1}), with four samples.
  • ⋮ ⁡
  • The resolution level 0, l0 = DWT−1({l1,h1}).

1.4 Orthogonal and biorthogonal DWT

All DWTs are deﬁned by 4 ﬁlters (using a similar notation that the followed by PyWavelets):

  1. Analysis (downsampling) low-pass ﬁlter ϕ~ (the decomposition scaling function), which computes ls+1 from ls.
  2. Analysis (downsampling) high-pass ﬁlter ψ~ (the decomposition wavelet function), which computes hs+1 from ls.
  3. Synthesis (upsampling) high-pass ﬁlter ϕ (the reconstruction scaling function), which computes {l2ns} from ls+1 and hs+1.
  4. Synthesis (upsampling) high-pass ﬁlter ψ (the reconstruection wavelet function), which computes {l2n+1s} from ls+1 and hs+1.

In the context of the DWT, orthogonality provides intersubband decorrelation, which basically means that the contribution of each subbands to the reconstruction (and this holds for all DWTs) of the signal are independent. Orthogonal ﬁlters can be recognized because:

  1. ϕ~⊥ψ~ and ϕ⊥ψ, where ⊥ denotes orthogonality.
  2. Their taps are all diﬀerent (the ﬁlters are asymmetric).
  3. The taps of the synthesis ﬁlters can be determined simply by “reﬂecting” the taps of the analysis ﬁlters.
  4. In general, except for the Haar DWT, orthogonal ﬁlters generate phase distortion.

On the other hand, in a biorthogonal DWT:

  1. ϕ~⊥ψ~, ϕ⊥ψ, ψ⊥ϕ~ and ψ~⊥ϕ.
  2. All the ﬁlters are symmetric.
  3. The ﬁlters satisfy that
    ψ = (−1)nϕ~; n ∈ ℕ, (13)

    and

    ϕ = (−1)nψ~; n ∈ ℕ (14)

    (the odd taps are multiplied by − 1). Therefore, if we have the analysis ﬁlters we can deduce the synthesis ﬁlters and viceversa.

  4. No phase distortion is generated.
