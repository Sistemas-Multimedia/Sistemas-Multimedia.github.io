1 Description

1.1 Introduction

The main objective of this milestone is to remove the intercomponent redundancy that most of natural images exhibit, and to concentrate the energy of the signal.

In general, redundancy in signals is usually expressed as a correlation dependency between samples. In the case of an image (and of course, of a video), a pixel (a sample) is represented usually with three color components (or channels), each one measuring the energy in a diﬀerent band of the visible spectrum, and one source of redundancy is the possible correlation between those color components.

To estimate the redundancy there are basically two options:

  1. To compute the 0-order (memoryless sources) entropy of the signal: the higher the entropy, the lower the redudancy. In fact, if we suppose that the samples of the signal are uncorrelated, the 0-order entropy is an exact mesarure of the expected bit-rate achieved by an arithmetic encoder. Unfortunately, the 0-order entropy is usally a lower bound for the estimation of the redundancy.
  2. A better way is to use an lossless compressor: the higher the length of the compressed ﬁle compared to the length of the original ﬁle, the lower the redundancy.1 Notice, however, that although this estimation is more accurate than the entropy, in general, it depends on the encoding algorithm (diﬀerent algoritms can provide diﬀerent estimations).

In this milestone we are going to remove the intercomponent redundancy of a video frame X, i.e., the redundancy that may exist between the color components of each pixel of X. The color spaces that we are goint to compare are: (1) RGB, (2) the YCrCb, and (3) the YCoCg.

1.2 Some words about transform coding

Quantization is more eﬀective when the energy of the signal is accumulated in an small number of samples. Transform coding can exploit correlation in signals to concentrate the energy. All linear2 transforms can be described by matrix-vector product [3] as

y = Kx, (1)

where x is the input signal, K is the analysis transform matrix, and y is the output decomposition. Notice that

yi = ⟨Ki,xi⟩, (2)

where Ki is the i-th row of K, and ⟨⋅,⋅⟩ denotes the inner product. This basically means that yi is proportional to the similarity between the input signal x and the taps of the ﬁlter Ki. These slides can help you with this key idea. The inverse (synthesis) transform is computed by

x = K−1y, (3)

where K−1 denotes to the inverse matrix of K. For example, the 2x2-KLT [2] is deﬁned by

[ y0 y1 ] = [ 1 2 1 2 1 2 −1 2 ] [ x0 x1 ] , (4)

and it holds that

K = K−1 = KT, (5)

where KT represents the transpose matrix of K. The Eq. 5 is true for all orthogonal transforms, and therefore

⟨Ki,Kj⟩ = 0,∀ ⁡i≠j. (6)

1.3 The RGB/YCrCb transform

1.3.1 Analysis and synthesis

To convert a (color) pixel from the RGB into the YCrCb domain, we use the RGB/YCrCb (analysis) transform [1]

[ Y Cr Cb ] = [ 0.299 0.587 0.114 0.5 −0.4187 −0.0813 −0.1687 −0.3313 0.5 ] [ R G B ] , (7)

where Y is the luminance (luma) component and CrCb is the crominance (chroma). The main reason for such a mapping is that the HVS (Human Visual System) is much less sensitive to high-frequency components in chroma. Thus, compression systems such as JPEG can downsample the chroma components (usually by 2:1 in each of the horizontal and vertical directions), as well as increase their quantization step sizes with respect to luma, to achieve further compression.

Eq. 7 can be also written as

Y = 0.299R + 0.587G + 0.114B Cr = 0.713(R −Y) + δ Cb = 0.564(B −Y) + δ, (8)

where,

δ = { 128 for 8 bits (unsigned) images, 32768 for 16 bits (unsigned) images, 0.5 for ﬂoating point ([0,1]) images. (9)

is used to avoid negative components3 . As it can be seen, Cr and Cb are scaled versions of R −Y and B −Y, so Cr and Cb can be interpreted as measures of how much red and blue content in a pixel diﬀers from luma, respectively. Notice also that for a gray pixel, R = G = B = Y, and so Cr = Cb = 0 [1].

The inverse (synthesis) transform is deﬁned by

[ R G B ] = [ 1 1.402 0 1 −0.714 −0.344 1 0 1.772 ] [ Y Cr Cb ] , (10)

or alternatively, by

R = Y + 1.403(Cr − δ) G = Y − 0.714(Cr − δ) − 0.344(Cb − δ) B = Y + 1.773(Cb − δ). (11)
1.3.2 Quantization

After analyzing the frame (representing it in the YCrCb domain), the next natural step is quantization. Supposing that we will use a static uniform dead-zone quantizer with quantization steps ΔY, ΔCr, and ΔCb, for the coeﬃcients Y, Cr, and Cb, repectively, and under the assumption of that the RGB/YCbCr is an orthogonal transform and that each channel is compressed independently, the optimal quantization of the channels should use ΔY, ΔCr, and ΔCb so that

λY = λCr = λCb, (12)

where λchannel(R) is the slope, for a given bit-rate R, (Rate-Distortion) of the curve for the corresponding channel in the RD plane (see this notebook) [4, 2].

Unfortunately, the use of Eq. 12 implies the computation of the RD curve for each channel, which is a time-consuming operation. For this reason, and supposing that the statistics of each channel are similar, we can suppose that the length (the bit-rate) of each channel is proportional to the variance of the channel, which is proportional to the quantization step. Therefore, if all the channels have the same gain4 , a quantization strategy that should approximate Eq. 12 is to use

ΔY = ΔCr = ΔCb. (13)

When the gains are not the same, the quantization steps should be divided5 by the gains, that for the RGB/YCrCb transform are:

Y : 12 + 12 + 12 = 3 Cr : 1.4022 + 0.7142 = 2.4754 Cb : 0.3442 + 1.7722 = 3.25832

Unfortunately, the RGB/YCrCb transform is not orthogonal. This means that the quantization noise introduced in one of the channel will also aﬀect to the rest of channels, which will degrade the RD performance. The lack of orthogonality also reduces the eﬀectivity of the previous algorithm for determining the optimal quantization steps.

1.4 The RGB/YCoCg transform

1.4.1 Analysis and synthesis

Clearly, orthogonality is a desired property in compression systems. On the other hand, Eqs. 7 and 9 were derived by Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on old6 video data. The same procedure has been carried out with newer7 images, obtaining

[ Y C 1 C2 ] = [ 1 3 1 3 1 3 1 2 0 −1 2 −1 4 1 2 −1 4 ] [ R G B ] ⇔ [ R G B ] = [ 1 1 −2 3 1 0 4 3 1 −1 −2 3 ] [ Y C 1 C2 ] . (14)

This transform is orthogonal. However, as [1] points it has to disadvantages:

  1. The gains of the channels are diﬀerent (2, 3 and 8/3).
  2. From a perceptual perspective, the inﬂuence of the green channel on the luma channel should be stronger.

For these reasons, [1] proposes

[ Y Co Cg ] = [ 1 4 1 2 1 4 1 2 0 −1 2 −1 4 1 2 −1 4 ] [ R G B ] ⇔ [ R G B ] = [ 1 1 −1 1 0 1 1 −1 −1 ] [ Y Co Cg ] . (15)
1.4.2 Quantization

As it can be seen, the RGB/YCoCg transform is orthogonal and the channels gains are the same. Therefore, the Eq. 13 stands Eq. 12. As it can be also seen in this notebook, the YCoCg domain can signiﬁcantly concentrate the energy of the pixels in the Y channel. This is beneﬁcial because quantization will remove (completely quantize) “noise” in the chroma channels.
